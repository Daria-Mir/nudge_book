Meet the Choice Architects

Richard H. Thaler

Nobel Laureate in Economics

Richard H. Thaler was awarded the 2017 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for his pioneering contributions to the field of behavioral economics. He is the Charles R. Walgreen Distinguished Service Professor of Behavioral Science and Economics at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business. He is a member of the National Academy of Science and the author of Misbehaving: The Making of Behavioral Economics.

Cass R. Sunstein

Legal Scholar & Public Advisor

Cass R. Sunstein is the Robert Walmsley University Professor at Harvard University and the founder and director of the Program on Behavioral Economics and Public Policy at Harvard Law School. From 2009 to 2012 he served in the Obama administration as administrator of the White House Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs. In 2018 he received the Holberg Prize, the equivalent of a Nobel Prize for law and the humanities.

The Storybook Journey

Act I: Humans vs. Econs

Act 01

Standard economics assumes we are Econs—logical machines that calculate utility instantly and never blunder. But in reality, we are Humans. Our minds operate using a dual-system cognitive structure:

System 1 (Automatic) is rapid, emotional, and handles 95% of our actions. System 2 (Reflective) is slow, logical, and deliberate. Because System 2 is lazy, we rely heavily on System 1 shortcuts.

This reliance leaves us highly vulnerable to predictable blunders: Anchoring (adjusting decisions from an initial arbitrary value), Availability bias (overestimating risks based on vivid memories), and Status Quo Bias (our overwhelming tendency to stay put and take the default option).

Act II: The N.U.D.G.E.S. Blueprint

Act 02

If absolute neutrality is a myth, choice architecture is inevitable. The layout of items in a supermarket, options on a health insurance form, or default settings in an operating system all govern choices.

To design choices responsibly, the authors present the **N.U.D.G.E.S. framework**:

- iNcentives: Align incentives with user goals.
- Understand mappings: Translate complex variables so citizens can compare them.
- Defaults: Set intelligent pre-selections since many users will accept whatever is pre-checked.
- Give feedback: Provide visible confirmation or correction.
- Expect error: Allow grace for human mistakes.
- Structure complex choices: Break massive options down into pathways.

“If you want people to do something, make it easy.”

— Richard H. Thaler

Act III: Savings, Debt & Markets

Act 03

Money is a battlefield of cognitive errors. Econs calculate compounding interest and stock portfolio variances flawlessly. Humans struggle with credit card minimum payments and put off saving for retirement because of loss aversion (our paycheck feels depleted).

To fix retirement, Thaler co-created Save More Tomorrow (Sart):

- **Enrollment is automatic** (making status quo bias work for you).
- **Contributions only escalate when you get a raise** (avoiding immediate loss aversion).
- **Escalation keeps repeating** until a cap is met, locking in dynamic savings.

In banking and mortgages, Borrow More Today is driven by high-friction credit structures, hidden fees, and complex interest options. Smart disclosures are required to level the playing field.

“Loss aversion is a fact of human nature. By planning for it, we can design savings that grow automatically.”

— Richard H. Thaler

Act IV: Organ Donations & Planet Earth

Act 04

In society and health, default states determine human lives. When registering for a driver's license, the consent rate for organ donation is highly dependent on whether registration is Opt-In or Opt-Out.

Opt-in defaults (where you must check a box to donate) result in consent rates around 12–15%. Opt-out defaults (presumed consent, where you are enrolled unless you tick a box) result in rates over 99%. A tiny box on a DMV sheet alters the availability of thousands of organs.

For our environment, Green Nudges use similar defaults. Automatically enrolling homes in renewable electricity plans (while leaving opt-out free) is more effective than any green advertising campaign. Real-time feedback displays in homes also trigger conservation habits.

“There is no such thing as a neutral design. Choice architecture is inevitable.”

— Cass R. Sunstein

Act V: Sludge, Ethics & Future

Act 05

Not all choice architecture is benevolent. The authors introduce Sludge—friction, complex forms, and administrative wait-times designed to prevent citizens from getting aid or casting votes.

To counter sludge, governments require **Smart Disclosure** (making terms clear, machine-readable, and easily comparable) and aggressive **Sludge Audits** to clear red tape.

Ethically, nudging must follow **libertarian paternalism**: design choices to help people make better decisions (paternalism) while making opt-out free, transparent, and easy (libertarian). Nudges must never be deceptive.

“Nudges are not mandates. A nudge is any aspect of the choice architecture that alters behavior without forbidding any options.”

— Nudge: The Final Edition
